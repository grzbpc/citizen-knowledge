**A £10bn waste figure that answers a narrower question than it looks like**
*DEEP READ ENTRY*
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 13: Discretion as Power → The Cost of Enforcement*

Metro reports on Baroness Hallett's Covid inquiry findings: almost £10billion of the £14.9billion spent on PPE during the pandemic was wasted, through overbuying and the purchase of substandard equipment that put health and care staff at risk. The report heavily criticised the fast-tracked "VIP lane," which gave priority for contracts to companies with links to the then Conservative government. Naomi Fulop of Covid-19 Bereaved Families for Justice said her mother's care staff were expected to wear one thin medical mask for an eight-hour shift, and believes that is how she contracted Covid and died. Hallett's report found "embedded unfairness" in the system that undermined public trust; findings on PPE Medpro, the company linked to then-Tory peer Michelle Mone, will follow any criminal proceedings.

The headline figure, £10billion wasted, is the kind of number that invites a straightforward reading: emergency procurement under pressure, mistakes made at speed, money lost to chaos rather than design. Hallett's report doesn't really support that reading. The waste sits downstream of a specific structural choice, the VIP lane, which routed some suppliers past the ordinary scrutiny others had to clear. **A flood-risk rule that survives by redefining what counts as warning**
*Maps to: Part I: When the Law Stops Meaning What It Says → Chapter 1: The Illusion of Law*

Metro reports that approval has been given to build 190 homes on land in Yatton, Somerset, carrying the highest level of flood risk, on the basis that residents would have 42 hours to evacuate if sea defences were breached. North Somerset Council challenged the decision, but the High Court backed planning inspectors, ruling that residents "would not be taken by surprise."

Flood-risk zoning exists to mean something specific: land above a certain risk threshold shouldn't be built on, because the consequences of getting it wrong are severe and largely irreversible once 190 households are living there. That's the rule as stated. What actually happened is that the rule was satisfied not by reducing the risk, but by redefining what "acceptable" means at the highest risk tier: not "safe," but "residents would have notice." A 42-hour evacuation window became the mechanism by which the highest flood-risk classification in the country was rendered buildable.

This is the same pattern Chapter 1 traces through speed limits and their real operating threshold, just moved into planning law. The rule on paper stays intact, unchanged, still describing itself as protective. What shifts is the practical test applied underneath it, quietly, at the point of enforcement, until "highest risk" and "buildable" turn out not to be contradictory after all. The council's legal challenge is itself worth noting: this wasn't an oversight nobody caught. The system saw the objection clearly stated and ruled against it anyway, which tells you the redefinition wasn't a loophole slipping through unnoticed. It was upheld as the correct reading of what the rule requires.

**The same human rights protection, read completely differently depending on who invokes it**
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 12: Illegal, Illicit, and Ignored → The Gap Between Law and Practice*

Metro reports that Aigars Balsevics, 45, former mayor of Wisbech, Cambridgeshire, jailed for six-and-a-half years in 2023 for raping a woman twice at his residence in 2021, will be deported to Latvia after his sentence. He had argued that sending him back would breach his human rights, since it would harm his relationship with his two children. An Upper Tribunal judge ruled that argument was outweighed by "the nature and seriousness of his offending."

The right to family life under human rights law is a real, standing legal principle, and this case shows it functioning exactly as designed: invoked, weighed seriously, and then outweighed by the gravity of the offence. Nothing procedurally wrong happened here. That's precisely what makes the case useful, because the same right, argued in other deportation cases that receive far less scrutiny, is frequently reported as though its mere invocation is itself the scandal, an abuse of a system too generous to people who don't deserve it.

Chapter 12's discussion of the gap between what a law says and how it's publicly understood to function applies directly. A legal protection exists on paper, uniformly, for anyone it covers. What varies enormously is which invocations of it get reported as reasonable judicial weighing, as happened here, and which get reported as a system rigged in favour of people who shouldn't be protected by it at all. The mechanism generating those different reactions has very little to do with the law itself, which operated identically in both kinds of case. It has to do with who is asking, and how sympathetically that person's claim gets framed before a reader ever gets to the legal reasoning underneath it.

**Safety measures that arrive after the case that makes the news, not before**
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 13: Discretion as Power → Cost Without Resolution*

Metro reports that 4,011 sex offences have been recorded at hotels across England over the past three years, based on figures from 27 police forces (a dozen forces could not provide data, meaning the true number is higher). The figures come months after Kyran Smith, 29, was jailed for seven-and-a-half years for lying to obtain a key at a Travelodge in Maidenhead and attacking a woman in her room, a case that prompted industry chiefs to introduce a raft of new measures to protect vulnerable guests. Multiple women told Metro the problem was not an isolated incident: one described staff "downplaying" a terrifying situation at a Premier Inn, another was given a key to a room she was showering in because staff believed it was empty.

Reported offences rose 6.5% in a single year, across a system that, by its own industry body's admission, only introduced meaningful protective measures after one case became public enough to force the question. That sequencing matters. The underlying vulnerability, no phone in some rooms, no reliable way to verify a guest's identity before issuing a key, staff minimising serious complaints, was not new information produced by the Smith case. It was the operating condition the whole time, absorbed as background risk until one incident became visible enough to require a response.

Chapter 13's account of enforcement cost without resolution describes this kind of gap precisely: not an absence of the tools or awareness needed to prevent harm, but tools and awareness that exist in principle while sitting unused until forced into practice by a single case that happens to break through. The industry's new measures are a real response, and worth acknowledging as such. But "prompted by" is the operative phrase. What made the difference wasn't new evidence of risk, since Bartlett's and Cousins' accounts show that risk was already lived and already reported to staff in real time. What made the difference was that one case became public enough that inaction stopped being a viable option. Everyone the system failed before that point was, on the evidence here, failed by a system that already had what it needed to know better.

**A closed investigation, sitting quietly next to a pay rise**
*BRIEF*
*Maps to: Part III: Moral Inversion → Chapter 8: Work as Virtue, Poverty as Failure*

Metro's BBC pay-list report notes, almost in passing, that Scott Mills, the corporation's highest-paid star until his sacking in March, had an internal Metropolitan Police-prompted investigation opened into serious allegations involving a boy aged under 16, between 1997 and 2000. Prosecutors decided in 2019 there was insufficient evidence to bring charges, and the file was closed. Mills earned between £745,000 and £749,999 in the year to March 2026.

"Insufficient evidence to charge" is a specific, narrow finding, not an exoneration, and institutions are generally careful to keep those two things separate when it suits them. What's notable here is how little the 2019 closure appears to have shaped anything afterwards: the salary continued rising for years after the file closed, and the sacking in March is reported without a clearly stated cause. An institution can hold a closed file and a rising pay grade in the same hand indefinitely, right up until something forces the two into the same sentence.

**A law built because the ordinary process didn't work**
*BRIEF*
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 13: Discretion as Power*

Incoming PM Andy Burnham told the Commons the Hillsborough Law, imposing a duty on public officials to tell the truth and cooperate with investigations, will "reshape the relationship between the public and the state." It takes its name from the 1989 disaster in which 97 Liverpool fans died, and follows 37 years in which, Burnham wrote, families "stood firm when powerful institutions closed ranks against them."

A new law compelling officials to cooperate with inquiries is only necessary because the existing legal and institutional framework didn't require that, in practice, for 37 years. Officials weren't breaking the law by closing ranks; there wasn't a law making that illegal. This is worth including as the other side of the book's usual pattern: sometimes the gap between rule and practice does eventually get closed, but only after decades, and only because the people harmed refused to stop pointing at it. The law itself is a late admission of exactly what its absence had already made clear.

_This entry shows a new legal duty as a late admission: officials were never required to cooperate honestly with inquiries, and thirty-seven years of that gap is what finally produced the law._
