**A crackdown announced after the cases it responds to are already over**
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 13: Discretion as Power → The Appearance of Action*

Metro reports that safeguarding will be strengthened at nurseries after "appalling" abuse cases, with early education minister Olivia Bailey saying the government is "absolutely determined" to protect children. Bailey cited nursery workers Vincent Chan, jailed for 18 years for a campaign of sexual abuse, and Nathan Bennett, jailed for 24 years for raping and abusing young children in his care, alongside the case of nine-month-old Genevieve Meehan, killed by a worker who strapped her face-down on a bean bag for 90 minutes. Proposed changes include mandatory CCTV under consideration, powers for Ofsted to act across whole provider chains rather than individual settings, and mandatory staff supervision.

Every measure being proposed here is reasonable on its own terms. CCTV, chain-wide inspection powers, mandatory supervision: nobody would argue these are bad ideas. What is worth noticing is the sequence. The children these measures are meant to protect have already been harmed. The convictions have already happened. The system is being strengthened in direct, named response to specific catastrophic failures it did not prevent, which means the announcement is not really describing prevention at all. It is describing repair, dressed in the language of getting ahead of a problem.

Chapter 13's account of what enforcement optimises for is relevant here: a system under this kind of pressure tends to produce visible, nameable action rather than the harder, slower work of actually closing the gap that let Chan and Bennett operate for as long as they did. Mandatory CCTV and chain-wide powers are the kind of reform that photographs well and can be pointed to in a press release. Whether they would have stopped either case, or whether the actual failure was somewhere less visible, in supervision, in vetting, in how concerns raised by staff or parents were handled, is a separate question the announcement does not really engage with. Ms Bailey describing meetings with affected parents as harder than anything else in her life is not in doubt as a genuine reaction. But genuine horror after the fact and a system that reliably prevents the next case are not the same achievement, and the gap between them is exactly what gets papered over when a crackdown is announced in the same breath as the convictions that made it necessary.

**The word doing the work is "voluntary"**
*Maps to: Part V: Withdrawal → Chapter 14: Symbolic Politics and the Maintenance of Appearances → Statements as Evidence of Concern*

Metro reports that technology secretary Liz Kendall has announced voluntary social media curfews for 16- and 17-year-olds, preventing access between midnight and 6am, with addictive features such as auto-scrolling and algorithmic feeds turned off during that window. Teens will be able to switch the controls off if they choose. A government pilot of more than 300 teens found the curfews improved sleep and concentration, and found that 90% would not switch them off. The Conservatives called it a "dog's dinner." NSPCC chief Chris Sherwood labelled the plans "a sticking plaster" that fails to address the addictive design features driving high screentime.

The government's own pilot figure, that 90% of teens would not opt out, is being used as evidence the policy works. Read the other way, it says the same thing the NSPCC is pointing at: a meaningful minority would switch it off, and a curfew that can be switched off protects exactly the teenagers least likely to need protecting, while doing nothing for the ones the policy is nominally about. A genuinely at-risk teenager, in the grip of exactly the addictive design the NSPCC describes, is the person most likely to find the toggle and turn it back on. The 90% who leave it alone were probably closer to fine either way.

Chapter 14's discussion of symbolic solutions becoming attractive when practical ones are difficult applies directly here. Banning addictive design features outright, the auto-scroll, the algorithmic feed, is the practical solution, and it is notable that the announcement gestures at doing exactly that only inside the midnight-to-6am window, as though the addictive architecture becomes acceptable again at 6:01am. A curfew with an opt-out is not a weaker version of protecting children from addictive design. It is a different policy entirely, one that produces a statement of concern and a measurable pilot statistic without requiring anyone to confront the design incentives the NSPCC is naming. Minister Kanishka Narayan's own framing, that he wouldn't want to presume teenagers will all switch it off, inadvertently makes the point: the policy's success is being defined by how few people use the feature that makes it optional, which is a strange way to measure whether a protection is actually protecting anyone.

**Twenty-seven thousand password resets is what "secure" turned out to mean**
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 13: Discretion as Power → Cost Without Resolution*

Metro reports that two teenage hackers, Thalha Jubair, now 20, and Owen Flowers, 18, face sentencing after admitting conspiracy over a 2024 attack on Transport for London that a prosecutor said could have caused a potential £56billion loss to the UK economy. The pair spent six days inside TfL's servers using the log-ins of its chief information security officer, obtained after requesting a password reset, gaining what the prosecution called "the keys to the kingdom." They stopped contactless and Oyster payments, disrupted live Tube information, and viewed celebrities' account details, forcing TfL to pull the plug on its whole system and 27,000 staff to change passwords. The remedial work cost the public purse £29million. The pair, part of hacking gang Scattered Spider, face a maximum of 14 years in jail.

The prosecution's own language, "keys to the kingdom," names the actual finding here more precisely than the £56billion figure does. That number describes a hypothetical worst case the pair chose not to trigger. What they did trigger, a single password reset request against the login of TfL's own chief information security officer, is the more useful piece of information, because it is not a story about sophisticated infiltration defeating a hardened target. It is a story about a critical national infrastructure system whose security depended, at the point that mattered, on a process no more robust than the one an ordinary customer uses to get back into a locked account.

Chapter 13's discussion of dormant capability is usually about tools that exist but sit unused. This is close to the inverse: a vulnerability that existed, unused, until two people the prosecution's own framing repeatedly calls teenagers found it in an afternoon. The £29million remedial cost and the 27,000 forced password changes are the visible bill for a gap that was, on the specific facts here, cheap and simple to open. "Remarkably sophisticated, on a grand scale," as the prosecutor called it, describes the consequences accurately and the method less so. A system whose defences turn out to hinge on a single support-desk process is not a fortress that fell to a sophisticated siege. It is a system that had never been seriously tested until someone bothered to try the front door, and calling the result sophisticated is easier than admitting how little was actually required to walk through it.

**The same gap between a legal principle and a headline, a second time**
*Maps to: Part IV: Criminality and Control → Chapter 12: Illegal, Illicit, and Ignored → The Gap Between Law and Practice*

Metro's court report on the trial of Patrick Spencer, the 38-year-old MP for Central Suffolk and North Ipswich accused of sexually assaulting two women at London's Groucho Club, leads with CCTV showing him raising a clenched fist after one alleged assault, which prosecutor Polly Dyer suggested was "like a fist pump in celebration." Spencer disagreed, telling jurors "it's an interpretation but it's not my interpretation." He called himself "gregarious" and "huggy," conceded his hands accidentally touched one woman's breasts, and denied touching the other at all or asking either "lurid" questions about her marriage. He denies sexual assault. The trial continues.

This is worth naming plainly: the structure of this report is close to identical to Post 6, the court report on an actor's trial covered here in early July. A denial is included. The defendant's own account is given space. Nothing here is inaccurate reporting, again. And once again, the piece is built, entirely lawfully, around a single dramatic image, here a CCTV gesture and a prosecutor's characterisation of it, in a way that invites a reader to form a view well before a jury has reached one.

The reason this is worth returning to rather than treating as a separate, unrelated case is that the repetition is the actual finding. One instance of this structure could be coincidence, a single reporter's choices on a single day. A second instance, in an unrelated case, with a different defendant, a different court, a different alleged offence, shows something closer to a standing convention: this is simply how contested criminal trials get written up, consistently, regardless of the specifics. Spencer's own words to the jury, "it's an interpretation but it's not my interpretation," are doing more analytical work than they might appear to. He is describing, from inside his own trial, exactly the gap Post 6 named from outside one: a single ambiguous fact, filtered through one party's characterisation of it, hardening into the version of events that reaches print. The presumption of innocence still governs what the jury is legally required to do here. Whether it survives contact with a headline built around a fist-pump is, again, a separate question, and this book keeps finding the same answer to it in contexts that have nothing else in common.
