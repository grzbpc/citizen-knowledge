### The story

Metro reports that 4,011 sex offences have been recorded at hotels across England over the past three years, based on figures from 27 police forces (a dozen forces could not provide data, meaning the true number is higher). The figures come months after Kyran Smith, 29, was jailed for seven-and-a-half years for lying to obtain a key at a Travelodge in Maidenhead and attacking a woman in her room, a case that prompted industry chiefs to introduce a raft of new measures to protect vulnerable guests. Multiple women told Metro the problem was not an isolated incident: one described staff "downplaying" a terrifying situation at a Premier Inn, another was given a key to a room she was showering in because staff believed it was empty.

### The reframe

Reported offences rose 6.5% in a single year, across a system that, by its own industry body's admission, only introduced meaningful protective measures after one case became public enough to force the question. That sequencing matters. The underlying vulnerability, no phone in some rooms, no reliable way to verify a guest's identity before issuing a key, staff minimising serious complaints, was not new information produced by the Smith case. It was the operating condition the whole time, absorbed as background risk until one incident became visible enough to require a response.

Chapter 13's account of enforcement cost without resolution describes this kind of gap precisely: not an absence of the tools or awareness needed to prevent harm, but tools and awareness that exist in principle while sitting unused until forced into practice by a single case that happens to break through. The industry's new measures are a real response, and worth acknowledging as such. But "prompted by" is the operative phrase. What made the difference wasn't new evidence of risk, since Bartlett's and Cousins' accounts show that risk was already lived and already reported to staff in real time. What made the difference was that one case became public enough that inaction stopped being a viable option. Everyone the system failed before that point was, on the evidence here, failed by a system that already had what it needed to know better.
