West Mercia Police have started dressing officers up as joggers and sending them out to run the same routes, day after day, waiting to be beeped at, catcalled, or worse. When it happens, colleagues in a nearby vehicle pull the driver over. Six such operations have taken place so far, with harassing behaviour identified on three of the six occasions. Inspector Liz Warner says the aim is to send "a clear message" that "misogynistic remarks and predatory behaviours are not acceptable within our communities," and that the initiative may be rolled out more widely across Worcestershire.

I have no quarrel with the underlying aim. Horn-honking at women who are simply trying to run is a small, cowardly, and genuinely upsetting form of harassment, and I am glad it is being taken seriously. But it is worth pausing on the shape of the enforcement, because the shape tells us something the press release does not.

This is proactive, resourced, targeted policing. It requires officers to be taken off other duties, briefed, dressed, and driven out on a schedule, then paired with a second unit ready to intervene the moment an offence is caught on camera. It is expensive in officer hours relative to the offence itself, and it is aimed squarely at a change to the Public Order Act that only came into force in April, making it an aggravating offence to cause harassment, alarm or distress based on a person's sex.

None of that is wrong in itself. What it demonstrates, though, is capacity. West Mercia Police can, when the institutional will exists, build and run a bespoke sting operation for a single category of low-level public nuisance. That capacity does not appear from nowhere for six operations and then vanish. It exists, and it gets deployed, according to priorities that are set somewhere above the level of the individual officer.

This is precisely the mechanism I try to describe in the manuscript, that the appearance of a strict, universally applied law obscures a reality of continuous, unannounced choice about where enforcement effort actually lands:

> *The public is invited to believe that law enforcement is a fixed, mechanical process: an offence occurs, and the state responds. In practice it is something closer to a rationed good, allocated by decisions taken far from public view, and the citizen's experience of "the law" is really their experience of that allocation.*

I want to be careful here, because it would be easy — and wrong — to read this piece as an attack on the operation itself, or as minimising the harassment women describe. It is not that. It is an observation about what becomes visible when an institution does decide to act with this much deliberateness. The same police force that can find the officer-hours for six undercover jogging patrols is, like every force in the country, simultaneously making quieter, less publicised decisions about which other reported incidents do not receive that kind of resourcing. The sting operation is not evidence that enforcement is now comprehensive. It is evidence that enforcement is always selective, and that this particular selection has, this time, been made visible and given a press officer.

That visibility is itself worth noting. Most enforcement discretion is invisible by default — a case quietly not pursued, a report filed without follow-up, a caution rather than a charge. Here, for once, the institution has chosen to publicise its own discretion as a virtue: look, it says, at what we have decided to prioritise. It is a useful reminder that discretion is not hidden because it must be. It is hidden, usually, because publicising it would show the shape of everything it is not doing at the same time.
