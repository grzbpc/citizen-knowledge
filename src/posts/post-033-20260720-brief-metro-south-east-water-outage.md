About 7,000 properties across Kent and Sussex lost water supply after a shutdown at South East Water's Tunbridge Wells treatment works, days after the company was ordered to pay £30.5million for previous supply interruptions in the same region. The company's response was identical in substance to what a £30.5million penalty was supposed to prevent: an apology, and a statement that it is "working to resolve the issue."

This is the same South East Water named in [the hosepipe ban entry](/posts/post-09-hosepipe-postcode-lottery/) as first to impose restrictions on customers this summer, while its own network failures continue unresolved. A penalty of this size, imposed for exactly this failure, should function as a price on future non-performance high enough to force investment in preventing recurrence. Instead the recurrence arrived within days. Either the penalty was priced too low to change behaviour, or the underlying infrastructure problem is bigger than any fine the regulator is willing to impose, and either way, the accountability mechanism has, for the second time, produced nothing but an apology.
