### The story

London mayor Sadiq Khan has criticised Elon Musk and Reform UK leader Nigel Farage for what he says is exaggerating London's crime rates for their own political ends. Farage has described London as gripped by a "crime wave," while Musk has backed British far-right protests. Data released yesterday shows murder in the capital is at its lowest per capita rate in recorded history, making London safer than Berlin, Madrid and Paris and every US state, while "neighbourhood" offences including theft, personal robbery and vehicle crime have fallen 14%. At an event at Holborn police station, Khan said: "We know there are some people, for their own political agenda, who want to do London down, to create a dystopian image. This tends to be those who don't like the fact we are multicultural, progressive, liberal and incredibly successful." Metropolitan Police commissioner Sir Mark Rowley said: "The figures are clear, London is safe and getting safer each year."

### The reframe

Both sides in this dispute are pointing at the same official crime statistics. Neither side disputes the numbers themselves; the disagreement is entirely about what the numbers are for. Khan and Rowley present the falling figures as evidence that London's institutions are working and that criticism of them is bad-faith political theatre. Farage presents his own account of a "crime wave" without engaging with the published data at all, an assertion that exists independently of whatever the statistics show, because for his purposes the statistics were never really the point.

This is worth noticing precisely because it looks, on the surface, like a straightforward factual dispute that the data should be able to resolve. It doesn't, because statistics only settle an argument once both sides agree on what would count as winning it. Khan's argument is that London is safe, using a per-capita comparison against other major cities as the test. Farage's argument is about lived experience, visibility, and a broader sense of institutional decline, a claim that a falling murder rate does not directly address and was arguably never designed to be tested against. Sir Mark Rowley's flat assertion that "the figures are clear" is true of the figures and irrelevant to the argument actually being had, which is not about whether the numbers are accurate but about whose account of the city's legitimacy gets to be treated as the default one. That contest, over which framing counts as the serious, official version of events, is itself the more durable story here, and it will still be running long after this particular week's figures are superseded by next month's.

It is the same contest as this morning's entry on Andy Burnham's business rate cuts, where "social harm" was asserted by a minister as though it were a settled category rather than a chosen one. In both cases, an official framing is offered as the neutral, factual account, and the work of the framing is precisely to make its own selectiveness invisible.
