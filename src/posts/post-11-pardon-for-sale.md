### The story

NOTUS reports that rapper Boosie Badazz, facing sentencing on a federal gun charge, paid political operatives Jacob Wohl and Jack Burkman $600,000 last September after they claimed to have Trump "on speed dial" and could secure him a presidential pardon. On New Year's Day his lawyer was told the pardon had already been signed and was awaiting announcement. It never materialised, and a White House official said the clemency team had "never heard from" either man. Boosie is now in arbitration seeking to recover $300,000 under a refund clause in the contract. The same NOTUS reporting notes that Wohl and Burkman were previously paid $1 million to successfully lobby Trump for the pardon of a nursing home operator convicted of a $38 million fraud.

### The reframe

The formal pardon process exists to make clemency look like a reasoned, defensible exercise of executive judgement, weighing rehabilitation, sentence severity, and the public interest. What this story shows is a second, informal channel running alongside it, one that has nothing to do with those criteria and everything to do with who can pay for proximity to the person holding the pen. Boosie's case failed not because his underlying claim was weak by the standards the formal process claims to apply, but because the informal channel he bought access to turned out not to work. The nursing home fraud case shows the same channel succeeding for someone else, at a higher price.

That asymmetry is the mechanism Chapter 13 calls discretion as power: a decision nominally governed by process is, in practice, governed by access, and access has a market price that has nothing to do with the merits the process claims to weigh. The White House's denial that Wohl or Burkman had any real role is almost beside the point. Whether or not this specific pair delivers, the wider pattern reported alongside it, operatives being paid seven figures to successfully reach the president on clemency, confirms that the informal channel is real even when a given transaction inside it turns out to be fraudulent. A system that quietly rewards proximity while formally insisting on process cannot be surprised when a market grows up around that gap, nor when some of the people selling access to it turn out to be selling nothing at all.
