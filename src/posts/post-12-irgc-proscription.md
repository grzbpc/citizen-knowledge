### The story

Reuters reports that Britain has proscribed Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) as a terrorist organisation, along with a linked group, following a series of antisemitic attacks in London including the torching of four Jewish community ambulances. Prime Minister Keir Starmer said the new powers would "make it easier to prosecute and lock up anyone carrying out their dirty work here in Britain." Al Jazeera notes the designation, which the government had previously ruled out in order to preserve diplomatic relations with Iran, makes the IRGC the first foreign state agency to be classified this way by the UK, years after MI5 first acknowledged threats to kidnap or kill UK-based individuals linked to the organisation.

### The reframe

The capability to name and restrict the IRGC did not appear this week. The intelligence describing it as a threat to people living in Britain existed for years before the designation was made. What changed was not the underlying facts but the calculation of what proscription would cost diplomatically, weighed against what continuing not to act would cost politically once the attacks became visible and public enough, specifically once they reached synagogues and Jewish community targets in London, that inaction became harder to defend than the diplomatic fallout of acting.

This is the same structure Chapter 13 traces through the cold-case DNA sample sitting unused for thirty-six years: a capability existing in principle, available to be deployed, sitting unused for reasons that are rarely about whether the underlying harm matters enough to address. Here the capability was not technological but political; the tool was legal designation, not forensic science, but the shape of the delay is identical. The government's own prior position, that proscription would foreclose diplomacy with Tehran, was a judgement about relative costs, not a statement that the underlying threat was ever in doubt. What forced the recalculation was not new evidence of danger but a shift in what continued restraint would cost the government in public confidence. The people the IRGC threatened before this week were not protected by a system waiting for sufficient proof. They were living inside a system that had already made its assessment and simply priced the response as not yet worth making.
