### The story

Metro reports that Aigars Balsevics, 45, former mayor of Wisbech, Cambridgeshire, jailed for six-and-a-half years in 2023 for raping a woman twice at his residence in 2021, will be deported to Latvia after his sentence. He had argued that sending him back would breach his human rights, since it would harm his relationship with his two children. An Upper Tribunal judge ruled that argument was outweighed by "the nature and seriousness of his offending."

### The reframe

The right to family life under human rights law is a real, standing legal principle, and this case shows it functioning exactly as designed: invoked, weighed seriously, and then outweighed by the gravity of the offence. Nothing procedurally wrong happened here. That's precisely what makes the case useful, because the same right, argued in other deportation cases that receive far less scrutiny, is frequently reported as though its mere invocation is itself the scandal, an abuse of a system too generous to people who don't deserve it.

Chapter 12's discussion of the gap between what a law says and how it's publicly understood to function applies directly. A legal protection exists on paper, uniformly, for anyone it covers. What varies enormously is which invocations of it get reported as reasonable judicial weighing, as happened here, and which get reported as a system rigged in favour of people who shouldn't be protected by it at all. The mechanism generating those different reactions has very little to do with the law itself, which operated identically in both kinds of case. It has to do with who is asking, and how sympathetically that person's claim gets framed before a reader ever gets to the legal reasoning underneath it.
